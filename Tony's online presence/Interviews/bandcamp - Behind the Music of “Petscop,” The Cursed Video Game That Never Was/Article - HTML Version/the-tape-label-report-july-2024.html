      Welcome to The Tape Label Report, where we introduce you to five cassette-focused labels you should know about, and highlight key releases from each.

      Local labels like Anxiety Blanket Records are instrumental in helping establish and support artistic communities within the sprawling 4,000 square miles of Los Angeles. But the label also helps artists give back to the local communities they occupy through annual charity compilations, with proceeds being donated to organizations like Street Watch L.A. and Mutual Aid L.A.

      Anxiety Blanket Records launched in 2018, when friends Jake Tittle and Jeremy Leasure teamed up to produce a run of cassettes for Jeremy’s project, New Balance. Of the label’s name, Tittle explains, “Music can be that thing lifting the weight off of you, but the music is also holding you in a way.” The following year the pair released records from artists within their immediate social circles, like DCR Pollack and All Things Blue, saying they were “trying to create more of a sense of community, since L.A. is such a business town.” Since then, the label’s catalog has grown to include dozens of digital and physical releases on a variety of formats ranging from cassette and vinyl to VHS.

      Highlights for the pair include an early opportunity to work on the 7-inch release for Illuminati Hotties’s ppl plzr / post-everything and the label’s 2021 Charity Covers Compilation, which features local artists covering each other’s songs. “That felt like a really nice way to highlight how incredible a lot of the songs people are making are and that they are just as worthy of being covered as classics people are maybe more interested in doing,” says Leasure.

      While the label’s ethos is strongly influenced by DIY stalwarts like Drag City and early Sub Pop releases, the sound of Anxiety Blanket’s discography is more varied. “All of the artists on our label, when you listen to them, it feels like you’re being dropped into someone else’s world,” says Leasure.

      2021’s entry in the label’s Charity Covers Compilation serves as a good entry point into artists featured on Anxiety Blanket Record’s discography as well as other L.A.-based artists. A highlight pairing is Gracie Gray’s cover of the song “Come Home,” originally by Fell Runner.

      The song’s familiar, worn-in quality comes through on both interpretations, though they are stunningly different. While Fell Runner’s original version is carried by a guitar riff recalling “Walk On” from Neil Young’s On The Beach era, Gracie Gray transposes the song into a new key and adds some loud/quiet guitar dynamics to bring a more ’90s alt rock sound.

      -Zachary Vito

      London imprint Infinite Expanse began six years ago as a YouTube channel run by then-21-year-old Tom Gibbs to spotlight rare tapes and deep cuts across an eclectic spectrum of styles: Greek ritual rumblings, Detroit sci-fi electro, Italian planetarium library music, Estonian electronic waltzes, etc. Eventually, he realized that his musical curiosities merited a different, deeper outlet. “As much as anything, it seemed like a good idea to have something more substantial to show for all of the time I was spending finding this music,” says Gibbs.

      In keeping with the channel’s exploratory ethos (the name was chosen to “typify the never-ending supply of music out there”), Gibbs’s first foray into physical media was appropriately niche and mesmerizing: a reissue of a rare 1992 cassette by Czech multi-instrumentalist (and Blue Lizard label head) Jaroslav Kořán, Zahrada. A hushed, strange suite of pensive post-ambient improvisations, it was recorded at home “in peace and for fun,” with whispers of its intimate setting (kids, cats, birds) bleeding amongst the instruments.

      Since then, Infinite Expanse has released eight more cassettes and two LPs, in a widening spiral of unpredictable curations. Highlights include an archival gem by prolific post-industrial German duo Delta-Sleep-Inducing Peptide; a murky, experimental soundtrack to a 1985 Polish play about Auschwitz martyrdom; a reissue of Sydney-based ethnomusicologist Michael Atherton’s 1990 LP Windshift; and an expansive compilation of vintage ambiguities sourced from the 1989–1990 tape-trading underground.

      Gibbs’s gifts as both a digger and listener are evident in his catalog’s vision and variety, but it’s the human connection, as much as the sonic, that keeps him committed to researching and reaching out: “Some of the warm reactions I’ve received and the positive interactions thereafter is a big part of what it’s all about.”

      Soon to receive a vinyl edition, this 2023 cassette reissue of a 1990 tape by Czech Republic trio Richter Band is quietly mind-blowing. Soft slipstreams of crystalline guitar, clarinet, marimba, and “amplified clockwork” interweave with glassy percussive layers of the group’s signature instrument—the “fidlerophone,” an assemblage of jars played with plastic strainers that sounds a bit like singing bowls or a small gamelan. (The name derives from its inventor, Prague musician Luboš Fidler, who often collaborated with the band’s founder, Pavel Richter).

      Smetana is Czech for “cream,” which captures a sense of the music’s milky, meditative aura. Simultaneously minimal, diffuse, and devotional, the album fuses strains of sound art, New Age, and avant-garde improvisation into a liquid mosaic as effortless as it is evocative.

      -Britt Brown

      There’s something slightly cheeky about sticking the word “future” into the name of your tape label, considering the cassette tape’s permanent public perception as a thing of the past. Perhaps that’s where “obsolete” comes in—the tape was a futuristic medium once, just not anymore. In turn, Obsolete Future’s oeuvre is future music, just for an experimental timeline that has always existed separate from the mainstream.

      Confronted with a “dormant” local electronic music scene while living in Austin, Texas, label founder Conor Walker decided to fix it, albeit not by becoming a DJ. He admitted in a 2016 interview that he’s “a terrible DJ,” disinterested in “posh techno, singular styles and DJ etiquette” and partial to a mixing style that “[comes] across like a keyed lotto card without a single winning number, just metallic dandruff.” So he started his tape label instead.

      OF001, the minimal techno self-titled one-off Non-Volatile Memory, arrived in November 2012. OF002 would end up being Cyberia by Dyad, a retro electro LP featuring Denver musician and noted record nerd Charles Ballas, who co-runs the label. Since then the label has released contrast-heavy dub by Culprit, beguiling (and dulcimer-assisted) electro-acoustic from Long Meg & Her Daughters, and ambient by øjeRum so ponderous it feels tailor-made for a James Turrell installation. Florian Kupfer’s Observer is pitch-dark drone suffused with static. More recently, the label made a foray into the archival by releasing a compilation of free-flowing sonic sketches recorded in the late ’80s and early ’90s by Glasgow electronic legend Dave Clark under the name Oxide Youth. As it turns out, the underground past and the weirdos of the present are equally ripe with promise. Time is an illusion anyway.

      Dyad are among the more immediately approachable acts on Obsolete Future’s roster, and Channel 3 is balanced between the label’s ambient and experimental-leaning tendencies and the retro techno sparkle of prior release Cyberia. It maintains this balance by going everywhere all at once, and not without a sense of play (as in, the title track begins with the sound of a VHS being inserted in a player).

      It’s uninhibited but never overdone. Cyberia’s ‘90s acid sheen resurfaces on the ultra-bright and irresistible “Triton.” That’s followed by “Acid Syrto,” a trip-hop Jacob’s ladder so shambolic that you can’t quite call it a head-nodder. Longtime Denver rapper Kitty Crimes drops in for a feature on “Hazmat” to offer, “I’m off of blood, Dracu-lit.” Two tracks later, the trio has already returned to soppy ‘80s Italian film score synthwave on “Alloy.” It’s rare to hear all this so close together, and to hear it all done well. In other words, don’t touch that remote.

      -Elle Carroll

      When Paweł “Paide” Dunajko heard Chicago footwork for the first time in 2010, it transformed his life. “What brought me into footwork was its complicated sound, structure, and ornaments. This abstract and raw sound had a certain appeal for me. When I discovered that footwork is not only a musical genre but a whole culture centered around dance, it blew my mind,” he says. Almost nobody was talking about footwork in Europe at the time, but that soon changed. Dunajko formed the Polish Juke collective and label with Mateusz Kamiński. Soon they were organizing parties, inviting footwork pioneers like DJ Rashad and Jana Rush, among others, to Wrocław, and releasing albums by Polish artists. The country was becoming the genre’s hotbed—along with Japan.

      It was the Japanese offshoot of footwork that inspired Dunajko to create his own label, outlines. “I love their minimalistic approach and I think it’s a really original vision of footwork. I thought that creating a label exploring this particular aspect would be an interesting idea,” he says. He releases exclusively on cassettes as a tribute to early footwork and even though outlines sometimes move very far from the classic footwork sound, Dunajko still maintains relations with the Chicago scene members he met over a decade ago. “I still stress that the Chicago scene is the foundation of my work. It cherished tapes in the ’80s and the ’90s. I grew up in the same period and have an affinity for this format.”

      Each release in the label’s flagship groove series is made up of two nine-minute-long tracks as a way to free up footwork’s form. “I wanted to check how footwork would transform in this timespan, because normally tracks are two minutes long or even less,” he says. Groove releases come in three tape subseries, each with an overarching theme. These themes can be more straightforward, like Polish drummers or female producers, but they can also be more conceptual, like algorave or dub techno meets footwork.

      Only after devising a theme does Dunajko think about which artists to invite. “Footwork is a very flexible genre, but I always try to broaden it, to reach as far as possible,” he says. A good example of this approach is a tape by Hubert Zemler, a Polish experimental drummer who “tackled the idea of mixing dub techno and footwork in a very abstract, indirect way”.

      For a little over a decade Third Kind Records have been putting out cassette tapes of wildly inventive music spanning the spectrum from lo-fi singer songwriters to glitchy IDM and experimental sound art.

      Initially a partnership, the label is now a one-man band operating out of Brighton on the south coast of the UK. The man behind it all, Nick Langley, describes himself as “entrenched in Brighton for better or worse. I don’t think it has much in the way of scenes these days but you’re never too far from other creative people. People expect creativity here. I think that’s an encouraging environment to be in.”

      Appropriately, the label is a hotbed of creativity. Whether it’s Hattie Cooke’s bucolic electronica, the dub techno of Boodo Khan, Linden Pomeroy’s kaleidoscopic strumming, or tricksy turns from Embla Quickbeam and the mechanical techno man Graham Dunning, there are inventive and inspirational approaches as far as the ear can hear.

      Curating all these disparate styles is simple enough from Langley’s perspective: “It’s music that I want to live with. I’m not necessarily impressed by contemporary or cutting-edge sound; I’m asking, ‘Is it something I’ll still want to listen to 20 years from now?’” Discovering bands selling cassettes in 2013 inspired him to release this enduring music on a format with an equal history of longevity. “It immediately struck me as such a hopeful type of resistance to everything else that was going on in the music industry. I mostly still feel that way.”

      Up next for the label, Langley explains, there is “a Fisty Kendal mindisc, a new novel by Rupert Lally, and a record from Mike Paradinas, his first as Kid Spatula in 20 years. More tapes to come too though!”

      Langley’s own Reshapes, on which the label main man takes scissors and tape to diverse cuts from his roster, offers an ideal entry point into the idiosyncratic label.

      He applies his impeccable gauze to high energy workouts from Crushtrash, turns up the industrial clanging and heaving drones of Reflex Condition, transforms Superman Revenge Squad‘s dead crow soliloquy into a brain-lacerating set of twisting samples, and services Fisty Kendal’s “Orange Sodium Glow” with thick dub strokes and gasping pipes. Adding to the aural journey through his label’s trophy cabinet are swelling strings, woozy bell-bottomed psychedelia, a GBV-esque ode to manic putting, and flutes fluttering hopefully through an abyss, inviting listeners to delve independently into the Third Kind back catalog.

      -Jon Buckland
