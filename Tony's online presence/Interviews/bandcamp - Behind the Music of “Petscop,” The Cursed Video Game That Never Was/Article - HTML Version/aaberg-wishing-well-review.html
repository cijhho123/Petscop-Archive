      Googling Aaberg, the solo project from Philadelphia-based musician Hunter Mockett, doesn’t turn up much: just a few press clips and a Bandcamp page peppered with lowkey releases dating back to 2016. His social media presence is similarly understated with only a few hundred followers and a smattering of posted audio snippets laid atop dusky footage. Still, it’s hard to imagine Mockett maintaining such a low profile for long. Diving into his work offers a glimpse at an artist with an enviable ability to douse wistful vocal melodies and dreamy guitar riffs in melty production. Mockett’s output twinkles and shimmers, evoking cut-out felt shapes dancing atop a navy backdrop.

      Mockett’s new Aaberg full-length, Wishing Well, arrives after a string of self-released albums and EPs. It’s issued by Forged Artifacts, a Minnesota indie rock institution that has carved a lane with records from acts including Half Stack, First Rodeo, and Ryan Pollie. In contrast to his newfound label mates, though, Mockett is most comparable to fellow Pennsylvania songwriter Greg Mendez. Both write songs that are as gut-wrenching as they are sentimental, perfect scores for honest self-reflection on verdant afternoons.

      Wishing Well lands somewhere in between the honeyed sounds of Bibio, The Radio Dept., and Elliott Smith. While Mockett embraced improvisation in the studio, the end result comes across as structured and compact, like a snow globe with a miniature suburban neighborhood housed within. On “SEA,” reverb-kissed guitar riffs interlock atop a bossa nova beat; “GULLS” is soaring and euphoric, thanks to the insistent clack of a cross stick snare and trebly fretwork; ambient closer “10 AM” plays into its daylit title, serene synth pads coated in quivering leads. “ICO” and “EMBRYO” are the gloomiest cuts on the record, carried by murky strums and downcast singing. “ACO” is earthy and muted, Mockett’s bittersweet intonations darting in and out of acoustic chords—Mockett’s soft voice smears into a captivatingly rich texture.

      Mockett recently moved from the UK to America, and Wishing Well was shaped by his adjustment to life in a new country. Although the music itself is warm and painterly, uncertainty underlines the record. It came to life atop a backdrop of interpersonal tumult and change and feels like a reprieve from that challenging period. This contrast beams especially bright on “MOUTH,” which uses aqueous metaphors to grapple with violence in Philadelphia. Wishing Well’s world is surreal and inviting, far removed from the turbulence of reality.
