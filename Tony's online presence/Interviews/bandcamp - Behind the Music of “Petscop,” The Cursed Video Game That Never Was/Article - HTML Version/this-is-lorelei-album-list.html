      LISTS A Mini-Guide to This is Lorelei By Mariana Timony · July 30, 2024
      Photo by Eve Alpert

      Nate Amos is jetlagged and fresh off a European tour with the much-feted Water From Your Eyes, his abstract, dancy pop duo with collaborator Rachel Brown, when we link over Zoom in late June to chat about his long-running, genre-agnostic bedroom solo project This is Lorelei, which at the time is about to release new full-length Box for Buddy, Box for Star on Brooklyn label Double Double Whammy.

      Box for Buddy, Box for Star is the first “proper” release for This is Lorelei in terms of receiving label distribution, a vinyl pressing, PR support, et cetera, but Amos has been putting out music under the moniker for years. He figures the formal start date to be 2014—at least that’s when the earliest Lorelei recordings began appearing on the project’s Bandcamp page: a sprawling collection of albums, EPs, and singles covering a remarkably diverse set of styles and approaches, from strummy, straightforward singer-songwriter fare and zippy pop-punk to goofy genre experimentations and a smattering of earnest cover songs, all self-recorded and self-released, usually within days of completing them.

      For the majority of its existence, This is Lorelei was little more than “a way to be creative,” says Amos, who would record while visiting his parents’ home in Vermont, during downtime between other bands, or just whenever. “It was very much just making stuff in my room then putting it online. None of these albums were things that were labored over for extended periods of time, they were just things that happened in these little moments. It always felt appropriate for this project to kind of document that because it’s like dating a diary entry or something. That’s really what this project was for a long time.”

      Musically, Box For Buddy, Box For Star is an amalgamation of This is Lorelei’s past, incorporating the styles Amos has experimented with over the past decade into a seamless whole where sincere country twang can sit comfortably alongside cheeky Auto-Tune. Often as laugh-out-loud funny as it is unironically vulnerable, Box For Buddy, Box For Star is a record of emotionally rich and musically complex love songs that retains a charmingly scruffy one-man-bedroom-project vibe albeit with more of a considered “album” flow than his previous releases under the Lorelei name, which often felt like (and were) one-offs and quick sketches.

      “I’ve kind of learned a little bit about navigating things with Water From Your Eyes, so I felt more comfortable approaching it,” Amos says of formalizing This is Lorelei for Box For Buddy, Box For Star. “Also, for this album in particular, it’s a very standard format ‘rock album’ or whatever. The idea of doing a more formal release with a vinyl and just a little bit more of an air of seriousness happened to fit really nicely conceptually with this album.”

      While Box For Buddy, Box For Star might serve as This is Lorelei’s “official” introduction a wider audience, Amos can’t help but see everything that led up to this point, warts and all. “There’s like a ‘Lorelei’ identity that I think has been found in the last two years so it kind of feels like a young project in that way, but it’s existed in various states for so long that it’s also this thing that’s always been with me,” he says.

      Below, the prolific Amos takes us through a few of these various states via selected records from the Lorelei discography that he sees as notable works within the larger project and shares some thoughts about each one, along with insights into his creative process and some advice for young artists who may feel overwhelmed about how to get started with Loreleis of their own.

      “That EP is what I have come to think of as the first Lorelei release for what the project has kind of become. That was the first time it felt like anything other than an experiment. It was very much like a practice and a training ground for other projects before that, then there was kind of a direction, which I got with that EP. The stuff I was making right before it was very inspired by modern classical and serialism and microtonalism [sic], a lot of other things that ended up taking root in Water From Your Eyes a little bit later.

      “Then I had a weird thing: I can’t remember exactly how it happened, but I got really, really fixated on ‘All The Small Things’ by Blink-182. I was like, ‘This is a perfect pop song.’ One of the marks of a perfect pop song is that you can do it in any genre and it’s just as good. I went through a two week phase where all I listened to was different versions of that song, every one that I could find. There are hundreds of them on Spotify and half of them are lullabies to put toddlers to sleep. But there are some really funny ones and there are some really good ones. I was in a zone where I was like, ‘Okay: fast, catchy, short, energetic, and get out.’ That was the starting point for that EP. Then it kind of changed a little bit as it went on. It got a little bit more melancholy in some ways. That’s not really where this project is at at this point, but that was the beginning of this kind of path.”

      So this EP was when it was like, okay time to write songs, not just experiment with sounds?

      “There were a lot of pop songs before that, too, but it kind of ran from pop songs straight into the ground and just became fully an experiment. A lot of stuff from that period of time I really like, but it does feel like a different project. So the whole thing kind of crashed and then this is what got up.”

      “If Move Around is the getting the ball rolling EP, this album actually feels like the debut album from the modern Lorelei period. I had just gotten really into Ween for the first time and the idea for [OK N8] being a 16-song album was because of Chocolate and Cheese. That album is 16 songs, and I really was impressed by the way it was all put together because you have these four movements of four songs. I really, really liked that format. So actually on OK N8, they’re in groups of four songs that run together with no breaks. The only time there is any silences is in between four and five, eight and nine, 12 and 13. I was definitely like a little bit in disaster mode personally, but I was really on a roll musically. Move Around was made with one song a day for seven days. I suppose just like, ‘Okay, I’m going to try and make 16 songs as fast as I can and see what happens.’ And I think it took 10 days.”

      The liner notes say it was recorded between August 1 and 11th, 2020—so this was during lockdown—and then it was released on August 12.

      “Yeah, there’s no time in between finishing and putting it out. That was the whole thing back then. I like that idea of fully diving into a space from it being absolutely nothing to it being publicly available, and then being able to look at it and say, ‘This thing didn’t exist in any capacity like two weeks ago.’ That’s kind of why this project became what it was, because I really liked that feeling. I thought it was fascinating because you’re not really any different than you were two weeks ago, but this whole thing came out. I kind of got almost addicted to that for a couple years where I pursued that at the expense of other things. It was a lot of fun. That period, like 2020 and 2021, feel like this weird musical bender in my head where there was so much stuff, because that was really the only thing I was thinking about. All the horrible things about it aside, I feel like the lack of pressure to be a social human being during that period of time kind of allowed me to thrive in some ways creatively.”

      “These were made in the exact same period of time. I think I was actually trying to do the OK N8 thing again with a 16 track album. Then I finished 10 songs and I was like, ‘I’m burnt out,’ so I just put them in an order and put them online. Then the next morning I woke up and I kept writing. The only reason those are separate releases was because I got antsy part of the way through and wanted to put some of it up. If I remember correctly, they were essentially made in the same two weeks. So they kind of go together for me, but also they do feel like separate things because when you put something out, even if you don’t stop before writing again, there’s a sense of like, ‘Now it doesn’t have to fit into that framework anymore.’ So, Stop Making Music is like an even further exaggerated version of Money Right Now. The whole thing was like, I want to make a songwriter album that’s the length of a grindcore album. So it’s 10 songs and 12-minutes long or something. There’s almost no real opportunity to communicate anything in a normal songwriting way, which made it really fun because there is no need to communicate anything. So it became largely wordplay and capitalizing on as many happy accidents as possible, typos and stuff. I was rearranging sentences and putting things in places so they would make less sense than they did the way I originally wrote them.

      “I think sometime months before I made these albums I got really into What the Brokenhearted Do by Tim Heidecker, which is essentially a fake ’70s songwriter breakup album that’s entirely fictitious. This isn’t even necessarily something that he does on that album, but it led to this idea of talking about completely non-dramatic things as if they were the most dramatic things. When you exist in this static state and you’re bored, you begin to make a big deal out of nothing all the time, and I think I was kind of thinking about that with those [albums].”

      Is humor something that’s important to you in writing?

      “Absolutely. I think that’s crucial to making music that deals with heavy topics in a way that isn’t just overly dour. If you want to really talk about anything, you have to shine a little bit of a light on it in whatever way. Then it’s also just contrast. Good drama is comedy to tragedy and every tiny micro location in between those things. If you’re not open to comedy, then you’re gutting half of the human experience that you’re trying to communicate in the first place. That’s not to say humor is critical to all art—there’s a lot of art where it doesn’t even factor in, but I think the idea of not necessarily making music but the idea of making pop music is inherently funny. The idea of standing in front of people with a guitar and being like, ‘This is a song I wrote about my feelings.’ No one can convince me that that’s not a funny thing to begin with.”

      Do you ever make yourself laugh when you’re making music?

      “Yeah, not even always because it’s funny but because I think it’s funny. A lot of my favorite music that I’ve made, I’ve laughed during the process while also being absolutely miserable during the process. I feel like if you can manage to make those things coexist or if you’re able to prompt that coexistence and reaction, even if it’s something that I made, at least that means it works for me.”

      Well, speaking of funny, this next record you sent over is very funny.

      “Yeah, that one is bizarre. Someone was like, ‘You should try and make a hyperpop thing’ and I was just like, ‘I’m too old to make hyperpop.’ Then I realized how funny it would be. I’m not going to manage to make hyperpop that could actually fit in with hyperpop, but if I just dive in and try and do it, it might be really funny. I like that one. It’s weird. It’s definitely an outlier for me, but I’d been sitting inside of Lorelei as a project being the new version of it that started with Move Around for long enough that I was like, ‘Okay, I think I’m comfortable just like doing whatever the fuck I want now.’ And it was cool. It was like a little phase. That’s another one that I think it got made in like a week. That’s part of what I like about making things and releasing them so quickly. If I tried to make something that week after that, it probably would have been totally different. That was the one period of time where everything could have led up to that EP existing.”

      Do you feel like now that you’re having a vinyl release and you’re doing interviews about it, that’s going to change the way that you approach this project? Like tomorrow could you still say, “I feel like putting out another This is Lorelei record. I’m gonna record it and just do it.”

      “I think that’s always how I’ll approach writing. I don’t think with this project I ever have any intention—I’m not interested in working on an album for a year. But if ultimately it helps it get to more people, I’ll let the release cycle take that long. So in a release sense, I’m willing for things to take longer, but I don’t think I could ever change the way I go about writing for this project just because it is so process-based. The reason everything ends up having a strong identity of its own is because of the density of the recording process. I think it would feel like a very different band if I spent a year working on a Lorelei album.”

      Does the success of Water From Your Eyes change the project at all? Does it become a release valve? Are there different expectations?

      “The main thing that’s changed is now Water From Your Eyes tours so much that I have to really capitalize on finding a period of time when I can do a Lorelei project, and it doesn’t happen that often. That actually makes it really compatible with doing longer album rollouts and working with labels. I could never have done that before because there was just way too much music. With [Box For Buddy, Box For Star], the main shift was, instead of being like, ‘Okay, everything that I make in this period of time, it’s making it onto this album,’ I actually worked really hard to cull the herd and turn it into the best album that could be rather than a snapshot of everything that happened in a particular period of time.”

      “That’s from right before Pa​$​$​ion Threshold, which makes Pa​$​$​ion Threshold even funnier to me. This does not include the album that’s coming out, but I think that [Falls Like Water Falls] is actually my favorite Lorelei album. It felt like a moment of calm in-between the whole Money Right Now/Stop Making Music thing and the Pa​$​$​ion Threshold thing. I worked on that one for a little bit longer. I think I spent like three weeks or something. I had stopped drinking before I made that, so I just smoked weed and worked on music all day every day and that album came together in a way that I like. Not including the one that’s coming out, I think I’m pretty confident saying that, out of that whole era, Falls Like Water Falls is the best one.

      You’re super prolific. For some people, it takes a long time to write a song or work on something and then put it out. Have you always just been musical? Even when you were a child?

      “I was never really trying to write music until high school, but I definitely was very into music. I took piano lessons and loved the Beatles, the normal things that people who end up writing songs do. I don’t know when the writing really fast thing started. I guess that was sometime like, mid-20s. At a certain point, I realized that things don’t have to be perfect and it’s probably better if they aren’t anyway. I got very good at putting things down and being like, ‘This is done.’ I used to overthink things a lot more and see things in a much wider space. As soon as I committed to the idea of snapshots of moments, then it kind of made it way easier to write really quickly, and be happy with things because regardless of how I feel about it now, in that moment it was the thing. That was very freeing, but it also was really stressful, because then you realize any moment that you’re not writing a song, you’re losing a song that you’ll never happen to write again. So I got very worried about that. I was definitely feeling pressure to be writing all the time because if I wasn’t writing, I felt like I was constantly throwing things in the garbage—which I’ve worked on a lot and I’m way more comfortable not writing now. But for a while, that was a very real item of stress for me.”

      There’s a lot of young artists out there that I think feel pretty overwhelmed by the idea of writing songs and putting them out into the world. Do you have any kind of advice, anything that helped you when you were first putting stuff out?

      “I would say, just do it. The real hurdle to get over is that nothing is ever going to be perfect. You can tweak things for years and years and years, and it still won’t be perfect. It’ll be different and maybe it’ll be different in a better way, but for me, learning to be like, ‘Okay, that’s good enough, that’s honest, it is what it is,’ and moving on was what allowed me to write as much as I did. That’s all practice. The more you write, the more you hone your craft. And the easier time you have calling things done, the more you’ll be able to write overall, which is ultimately more practice. You’re able to learn your craft at a faster rate if you’re just constantly doing. That worked for me, but that’s definitely not for everybody. It all depends on what you’re trying to do. I think, at least in the last five years, probably half of the songs I’ve recorded for Lorelei I haven’t put out. Just because you make something doesn’t mean you have to put it out, but the more you make, the more stuff you’re gonna end up with and the better you’re going to get.”

      You said that was something you had to work on. Was there any moment for you where you had to realize, I just have to not care?

      “Yeah, it was the Move Around EP. I guess that’s the difference between old Lorelei and new Lorelei: not giving a shit.”
