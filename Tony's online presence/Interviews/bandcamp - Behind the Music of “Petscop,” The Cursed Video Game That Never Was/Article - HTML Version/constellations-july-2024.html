      LISTS Constellations, July 2024 By George Grella · July 30, 2024

      Have you ever looked at an album you love and checked the credits, seen the musicians’ names on there, and wondered what else they’ve done? Following the social connections between musicians from record to record is the original version of what tech types call “music discovery”—and it’s still the best. While streaming services engineer algorithms with tools like machine learning and AI in order to take one track and send the listener to others, the results show that machines can’t listen and they especially cannot feel or make social connections.

      Follow one name to another and then another, though, and you’ve linked the paths of people who talk with one another and make music together, mixing their personalities and experiences, then go on to do that elsewhere. Follow the names because, like stars, they make constellations.

      In this bi-monthly column, George Grella gets out of the machine and back into the foundation of music making: people playing together. It’s the human connection—each song containing light to discover, each a place from which to see another light.

      This journey starts with Harriet Tubman, a great rock band that emphasizes that rock music is Black music, and that it can be as open and unbounded as free jazz while still rocking out. Guitarist Brandon Ross, bassist Melvin Gibbs, and drummer JT Lewis ground their playing in the blues and build outward with loping contrapuntal lines and polyrhythms that are part of global musical ideas, from African traditions all the way to Philip Glass.

      In Webber’s group is cellist Mariel Roberts, who has been a member of the Intentional Contemporary Ensemble and the Mivos Quartet, Bang on a Can All-Stars, and others. She’s also one of the leading solo performers in new music, and this track on her fascinating Cartography album pairs her with experimental composer Cenk Ergün in a duet that wrings as many different sounds as possible out of extended techniques.

      Ergün is the last step in these connections from rock to the edges of experimentalism. Alvin Curran is one of the great figures in this tradition from an earlier generation, and with Ergün on laptop and Curran at the sampling keyboard, they make a wild real-time collage of crashes, crunches, Beethoven, bangs, fragmented speech, machinery, and more, a bit of simmering old-time cartoon music popping in at times, like an emcee announcing the next performer.
